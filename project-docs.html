01

Data Foundation

The project uses the Olist Brazilian E-Commerce Public Dataset as a realistic e-commerce logistics structure. Olist provides orders, item-level freight, products, sellers, customers, purchase timing, order status, and estimated delivery dates. It does not provide real TikTok Shop data, real return requests, real insurance claims, expenses, claim development, or underwriting decisions.

The modelling value of Olist is structural: it contains the same kind of entity relationships that a return-shipping insurance product would need to price. The numerical rates in this project are therefore not production indications. They are evidence that the pricing architecture can be run end to end once real order, return, shipment, payment, expense, and claim data are available.

Data Audit Conclusions

  • All core joins are reliable: order items match orders, products, and sellers at 100%, and orders match customers at 100%.
  • The product category translation match is 99.96%; unmatched categories are handled as unknown rather than silently dropped.
  • Only product size fields have material handling needs, and even those are tiny: 18 missing rows, or 0.016% of the exposure table.
  • Freight and freight-to-price ratio have strong right tails, so pricing features use caps or log transforms rather than raw extremes.
  • Delivered exposures represent 97.82% of rows; non-delivered statuses are not mixed into the first insurance claim simulation.
Core limitation

Olist is useful for proving the data model and pricing workflow, but it cannot prove actual TikTok Shop profitability. Real deployment would require platform policy data, seller history, return records, carrier recoveries, expenses, claim development, and future-period validation.

02

Exposure Definition

The base insurance exposure is one seller's product line in one order. The project does not price at raw order level because a single order can contain multiple products, multiple sellers, and multiple freight charges. Item-level freight is the natural basis for return-shipping insurance because the insured cost is attached to the item/seller logistics row, not only to the order header. 

exposure_key = order_id + order_item_id + seller_id + product_id
Purpose

Align freight value, product attributes, seller attribution, and claim outcome on the same pricing row.

Output

One modelling row per order-item-seller-product exposure, preserving 112,650 unique exposure keys.

Validation

9,803 orders have multiple exposure rows, 1,278 orders involve multiple sellers, and one order has 21 item rows.

Why Order-Level Pricing Is Not Enough

  • Using order_id alone would blur multiple products into one risk row even though product category, weight, volume, and price differ by item.
  • Using order_id alone would misattribute multi-seller orders, which matters because seller credibility and seller monitoring are explicit outputs.
  • freight_value is stored in the order item table; pricing above that level would require arbitrary allocation back down to item/seller rows.
  • Exposure-level pricing can be aggregated upward to order, seller, category, route, or portfolio reporting without losing the original attribution.

Aggregation Logic

Level Use Reason
Exposure Model fitting, claim simulation, expected loss, and technical premium. The row contains the freight, product, route, and seller variables needed for pricing.
Order Customer-facing or seller-facing order premium display. Premium can be summed across all item exposures in the order.
Seller Credibility, loss ratio monitoring, watchlists, and rate action rules. Seller experience is useful only after controlling for exposure mix and sample size.
Portfolio Target loss ratio, total premium, rate adequacy, and stress testing. Insurance pricing ultimately needs aggregate expected loss and aggregate adequacy.
03

Synthetic Claims Layer

Olist has real e-commerce logistics fields but no insurance returns or claims. The project therefore builds a synthetic returns and claims layer to test the actuarial workflow. The synthetic layer is not presented as real market evidence; it is a controlled way to create frequency, severity, expected loss, and premium targets while preserving insurance discipline.

The first version deliberately prices only Coverage A: compliant return shipping. Failed delivery, replacement shipping, fraud, refund-without-return product loss, and broader logistics exceptions are documented as future coverage modules rather than silently folded into the current claim target.

Layer Representative fields Pricing meaning
Eligibility claim_eligible_flag, order_status Only delivered exposures enter the first-version return-shipping claim population.
Returns return_requested, return_approved, refund_without_return, partial_refund, return_reason Describes after-sale behavior. A return event is not automatically an insurance claim.
Claims covered_claim_flag, claim_type, gross_loss, recovery_from_carrier, paid_loss, net_loss Defines the frequency target and the severity amount used in expected loss.

Synthetic Assumptions

  • Eligibility is restricted to 110,197 delivered exposures; 2,453 non-delivered rows receive zero return and zero claim fields.
  • The base return rate is 8%, clipped between 1% and 25% after category, route, freight-ratio, size, and month factors.
  • freight_value is capped at its P99 value of 84.52 before severity generation to prevent extreme synthetic paid losses.
  • freight_to_price_ratio is capped at its P99 value of 1.549 before use as a frequency or modelling factor.
  • Product weight and volume missing values are filled with category medians, falling back to global medians when needed.
  • 5% of covered claims receive a carrier recovery, modelled as a small share of gross loss.
  • The random seed is fixed at 20260521 so the synthetic claim layer is reproducible.

04

Baseline Pricing

Baseline pricing establishes the portfolio rate level before multivariate modeling. It measures aggregate claim frequency, average severity, pure premium, and commercial premium under a 60% target loss ratio. It is intentionally simple: all eligible exposures receive the same benchmark expected loss, and non-eligible exposures receive zero premium in the first version.

pure premium = total net loss / eligible exposures commercial premium = pure premium / target loss ratio target loss ratio = 60%

Portfolio Benchmark

Metric Value Interpretation

Claim frequency 7.70% Covered claims divided by eligible exposures.
Portfolio pure premium 1.4802 Average expected claim cost per eligible exposure.
Portfolio commercial premium 2.4670 Pure premium divided by the 60% target loss ratio.
Total commercial premium 271,854.47 Total premium implied by the portfolio benchmark.

Observed Relativities

Baseline group summaries are not the final rate table, but they identify risk structure before modelling. Credible category summaries use at least 500 eligible exposures, route summaries use at least 500, and seller summaries use at least 100. These thresholds prevent small cells from being over-read as pricing factors.

  • office_furniture has the highest credible category pure premium at 2.1121, driven by high severity rather than high frequency alone.
  • SP_to_MA has the highest credible route pure premium at 4.1655, or 2.814 times the portfolio average.
  • Cross-state exposures have pure premium of 1.8644 versus 0.8024 for same-state exposures, confirming route and cross-state status as strong GLM candidates.
  • Seller baseline relativities are useful diagnostics but are not directly used as final prices because seller sample sizes are sparse and noisy.
Pricing arithmetic

A 60% target loss ratio is not the same as adding 40% to pure premium. If pure premium is 1.4802, multiplying by 1.40 gives 2.0723 and a 71.4% loss ratio. Dividing by 0.60 gives 2.4670, leaving 40% of premium for expenses, margin, capital, and risk load.

05

GLM Pricing

GLM is the champion pricing model because it is explainable, separates frequency and severity, and uses only quote-time-known variables. Seller ID and product ID are retained as identifiers but not used directly as sparse rating factors.

Allowed pricing features

product category, route state, cross-state flag, purchase month, purchase weekday, price, capped freight value, capped freight-to-price ratio, filled product weight, filled product volume, and estimated delivery days.

Two-Part Model Form

The GLM uses a two-part insurance pricing structure. The frequency model is a binomial GLM with logit link on covered_claim_flag across eligible exposures. The severity model is a Gamma GLM with log link on positive net_loss for covered claims only. Expected loss is the product of the predicted claim probability and predicted claim severity. 

glm_expected_loss_i = glm_frequency_i * glm_severity_i
glm_commercial_premium_i = glm_expected_loss_i / 0.60
Frequency model

Binomial GLM with logit link on covered_claim_flag.

Severity model

Gamma GLM with log link on positive net_loss for covered claims.

Expected loss

Predicted frequency multiplied by predicted severity at exposure level.

Validation Design

  • Train/test validation uses an order-level split so all item exposures from the same order stay in the same side of the split.
  • The split uses 80% of orders for training and 20% for testing with random_seed 20260521.
  • Low-exposure categories and routes are merged into Other below the 500-exposure threshold to avoid unstable coefficients.
  • Frequency validation reviews AUC, Brier score, actual frequency, predicted frequency, and calibration deciles.
  • Severity validation reviews actual severity, predicted severity, MAE, RMSE, and calibration deciles.
  • Pure premium validation reviews total actual loss, total expected loss, actual-to-expected ratio, and predicted pure premium.

06

Seller Credibility

Seller experience is added after the GLM, not inside it. This keeps the rating basis stable while still recognizing repeat seller experience when exposure count is large enough to be informative. 

Z = n / (n + 500)
seller relativity = Z x observed A/E + (1 - Z) x 1.0

Relativities are capped from 0.50 to 2.00 and normalized so total portfolio expected loss remains tied to the GLM expected-loss level.

Why Seller ID Is Not a Model Feature

The data contains 2,970 sellers with eligible exposure, but seller volume is sparse. The median seller has only 8 eligible exposures, the 75th percentile has 26, and only 29 sellers have at least 500 eligible exposures. Direct seller_id coefficients in GLM or direct seller_id splits in XGBoost would behave like noisy experience rating rather than stable insurance pricing.

Seller volume metric Value Pricing implication
Eligible sellers 2,970 Seller-level monitoring is needed, but direct modelling is high-cardinality.
Median exposures per seller 8 Most sellers need shrinkage toward GLM base expected loss.
90th percentile exposures 83 Even larger-than-typical sellers remain thin for direct experience rating.
Max exposures 1,996 Only a small group can receive material credibility weight.

Mechanics

  • seller_observed_ae is seller actual net loss divided by seller GLM expected loss.
  • Z = n / (n + 500), where n is seller eligible exposure count and 500 is the first-version full-credibility constant.
  • The raw relativity is Z * seller_observed_ae + (1 - Z) * 1.0.
  • The raw relativity is capped between 0.50 and 2.00 to avoid extreme seller adjustments.
  • After capping, portfolio normalization preserves total GLM expected loss, so credibility reallocates risk between sellers without changing total rate level.

Stress Testing

Stress testing measures the portfolio impact of adverse claim frequency and severity movements. The combined +20% frequency and +20% severity scenario pushes the loss ratio materially above target and is treated as a high-pressure scenario.

Scenario Loss ratio Interpretation
Base observed 59.37% Near target
Frequency +20% 71.25% Above target
Severity +20% 71.25% Above target
Combined +20% / +20% 85.50% High pressure

Scenario Design

  • Portfolio frequency shocks apply +10% and +20% claim count pressure to the entire eligible portfolio.
  • Portfolio severity shocks apply +10% and +20% average claim cost pressure to the entire eligible portfolio.
  • Combined shocks multiply frequency and severity, so +20% and +20% produces a 1.44 total loss multiplier.
  • Seller-tier stress applies +20% frequency and +20% severity only to one seller tier at a time, then measures portfolio-level loss ratio.
  • Watchlist category and route stress applies +15% frequency and +15% severity to selected high-loss-ratio segments.

09

XGBoost Challenger

XGBoost is retained as a challenger risk-ranking model. It slightly improves frequency ranking but does not replace the GLM champion because the pricing basis must remain explainable and calibration-led.

The challenger follows the same two-part insurance structure as GLM: an XGBoost classifier for covered_claim_flag, an XGBoost regressor for net_loss on covered claims, and expected loss equal to predicted frequency multiplied by predicted severity. It uses the same core quote-time features so that the comparison tests model form rather than hidden feature expansion.

Model Evidence Decision
GLM Test pure premium A/E 1.003 Champion rate basis
XGBoost Test AUC 0.5609; A/E 1.0145 Challenger score

Parameter Sweep and Calibration

  • The first XGBoost configuration was too flexible: train AUC 0.635 and test AUC 0.555 indicated overfitting.
  • A regularized configuration with max_depth 2, learning_rate 0.03, 500 trees, reg_lambda 5, and min_child_weight 20 improved test AUC to 0.561.
  • Class weighting was rejected for pricing because it pushed predicted claim frequency near 49% when actual test frequency was about 7.8%.
  • The calibrated challenger applies train-set A/E as a multiplicative adjustment for validation, then a portfolio calibration factor for the full-data diagnostic output.
Model Frequency AUC Pure premium A/E Loss ratio
GLM 0.5598 1.0030 60.18%
XGBoost calibrated 0.5609 1.0145 60.87%
Model selection conclusion

XGBoost helps ranking slightly, but GLM prices better. AUC alone is not enough to select an insurance pricing model because commercial premium depends on expected loss calibration, severity calibration, A/E, segment monitoring, and governance.

10

Interpretability

XGBoost interpretability is used to understand the challenger, not to overrule GLM calibration. The project uses built-in XGBoost gain importance and native SHAP-style contribution output from pred_contribs=True. The external shap package is not required in the current implementation.

Frequency Signals

The frequency model's strongest base-feature contribution groups are directionally plausible for return-shipping insurance. Category, cross-state movement, seasonality, freight-to-price ratio, product weight, route, price, and estimated delivery time all connect to return friction or logistics risk.

Base feature Contribution share Interpretation
category_group 25.74% Product category is the largest frequency signal.
cross_state_flag_cat 16.82% Cross-state logistics risk is material.
purchase_month_cat 10.10% Seasonality and campaign timing affect claim tendency.
log_freight_to_price_ratio_capped 10.06% High freight relative to item value changes return economics.
log_product_weight_g_filled 7.98% Product weight affects shipping friction and return behavior.

Severity Signals

The severity model is dominated by freight value, which is exactly what the current coverage scope implies. Coverage A pays return shipping cost, not product refund value, so severity should track shipping-cost proxies.

Base feature Contribution share Interpretation
log_freight_value_capped 80.69% Severity is overwhelmingly a return-shipping cost problem.

Interpretation boundary

The contribution tables explain how the fitted XGBoost model uses current synthetic data. They are not causal explanations of real TikTok Shop claims, and they do not make the challenger a production pricing model.

11

Model Governance

The production interpretation is conservative: the project demonstrates a complete pricing operating workflow, but the numeric rates are not production indications because the claim layer is synthetic.

  1. Use GLM expected loss plus credibility as the current champion pricing basis.
  2. Keep XGBoost as a challenger for segmentation, ranking, and exception review.
  3. Exclude outcome, post-claim, and synthetic-generation fields from feature sets.
  4. Monitor rate adequacy through A/E, loss ratio, seller tiers, and stress scenarios.
  5. Require real return, claim, expense, development, and validation history before production use.

Current Model Roles

Layer Role Governance position
Baseline Portfolio-average benchmark. Useful control, not sufficient as final segmented rate basis.
GLM Champion expected-loss model. Best current balance of explainability, calibration, and pricing discipline.
Seller credibility Post-GLM seller experience adjustment. Controls sparse seller experience without direct seller_id overfitting.
XGBoost Challenger risk score and nonlinear benchmark. Retained for governance and risk ranking, not selected as champion.

What Would Be Needed for Production

  • Replace synthetic claims with real policy, order, shipment, return, refund, carrier recovery, and claim payment records.
  • Create historical training windows and future validation windows rather than validating on one synthetic snapshot.
  • Add claim development, reporting lag, IBNR, expense load, commission, taxes, capital cost, and risk margin assumptions.
  • Validate feature availability at quote time and remove fields that are unstable, unavailable, or indirectly post-outcome.
  • Build monthly monitoring for A/E, loss ratio, claim frequency drift, severity drift, feature drift, seller-tier movement, and segment-level adequacy.
  • Define controlled rate actions: sample-size thresholds, single-change caps, elevated-tier watchlists, manual review rules, and pause/limit criteria.
  • Maintain champion-challenger governance: XGBoost could move closer to production only if out-of-time A/E, loss ratio, decile calibration, and interpretability are at least as strong as GLM.
Final technical recommendation

The project should be presented as a transferable actuarial pricing framework: exposure-level expected loss, insurance-style claim definitions, a transparent GLM champion, controlled seller credibility, monitoring, stress testing, and a governed challenger model. It should not be presented as a production TikTok Shop rate filing.
