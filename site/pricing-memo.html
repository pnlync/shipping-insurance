Pricing Memo · Actuarial Rate Basis

Return-shipping insurance technical pricing memo

A formal pricing-committee style memo for the current rate indication, coverage boundary, expected-loss method, credibility adjustment, monitoring evidence, stress testing, and model governance position.

Pricing recommendation

Adopt GLM expected loss plus seller credibility as the current technical pricing basis. The GLM provides the explainable base rate structure, and credibility adds controlled seller experience without allowing sparse seller history to dominate the indication.

The recommended commercial premium is calculated as credibility expected loss divided by the 60% target loss ratio. XGBoost should remain a challenger risk score and governance benchmark, not the champion rate formula, because its ranking improvement is too small and its pricing calibration is weaker than GLM on the test split.

Product and coverage scope

The priced coverage is Coverage A: return shipping. It represents the return shipping cost when a delivered order has an eligible, approved buyer return. The pricing unit is not a generic order, seller, or product prediction score; it is an insurance exposure carrying expected covered return-shipping loss.

The current version excludes product refund, refund without return, failed delivery, replacement shipping, fraud, platform-policy exceptions, and claim development. Those modules should be priced separately only after real operational data supports their frequency, severity, recoveries, expenses, and coverage rules.

Data limitation and exposure basis

The project uses the public Olist e-commerce dataset. Olist contains order, product, seller, customer state, route, freight, and delivery fields, but it does not contain real return-shipping insurance claims. The numerical rate indications are therefore framework evidence, not production TikTok Shop rates.

The exposure key is order_id + order_item_id + seller_id + product_id. This grain is necessary because freight value is recorded at the order-item level, and one order can contain multiple products or multiple sellers. Pricing above this level would require arbitrary allocation of freight, loss, and premium back to the seller-item exposure.

Synthetic claim definition

Because Olist has no claim table, the project creates a synthetic returns and claims layer. The design deliberately separates return behavior from insurance indemnity: a return request is not automatically a covered claim, and a covered claim is the event that drives frequency, severity, pure premium, and loss ratio.

The returns layer contains fields such as return requested, return approved, refund without return, partial refund, return reason, and request days after delivery. The claims layer contains covered claim flag, claim type, gross loss, carrier recovery, paid loss, net loss, and claim status.

Technical premium methodology

Baseline pricing sets the portfolio benchmark. The portfolio pure premium is total net loss divided by eligible exposures, and the commercial premium is pure premium divided by the 60% target loss ratio. This gives a baseline commercial premium of 2.4670 per eligible exposure.

The champion model is a two-part GLM. Frequency is modelled with a binomial GLM and logit link using covered_claim_flag. Severity is modelled with a gamma GLM and log link using net_loss on covered claims only. Expected loss is frequency times severity, and commercial premium is expected loss divided by target loss ratio.

The model uses quote-time-known fields only: category, route, cross-state flag, purchase month and weekday, price, capped freight value, capped freight-to-price ratio, product weight, product volume, and estimated delivery days. Outcome and synthetic-generation fields such as return_requested, return_reason, claim_type, paid_loss, net_loss, covered_claim_probability, and return_factor fields are excluded from modelling.

GLM pricing result

The GLM has moderate frequency ranking power, which is expected for sparse insurance claim events using only quote-time fields. The more important pricing result is aggregate calibration: test pure premium actual-to-expected is 1.003, which means expected loss is close to observed synthetic loss on the holdout split.

On the full portfolio, GLM expected total loss is 164,831.10 versus actual total net loss of 163,112.68. This implies a GLM actual-to-expected ratio of 0.990 and a GLM total commercial premium of 274,718.50 at the 60% target loss ratio.

Seller credibility adjustment

Seller ID is not included directly in the GLM because it is high-cardinality and sparse. The portfolio has 2,970 sellers with eligible exposure, but the median seller has only 8 eligible exposures. Direct seller coefficients would overreact to noise and create unstable rate indications.

Credibility is applied after the GLM. Seller observed A/E is blended with 1.0 using Z = n / (n + 500). Raw relativity is capped from 0.50 to 2.00, and then normalized so total credibility expected loss exactly preserves the GLM expected total loss. Small sellers are pulled back toward their own GLM exposure mix, not toward a flat portfolio average price.

Loss ratio monitoring

Monitoring compares actual loss, expected loss, and commercial premium. The two core metrics are A/E, equal to actual loss divided by expected loss, and loss ratio, equal to actual loss divided by commercial premium. Portfolio calibration is necessary but not sufficient; category, route, seller tier, and seller-level views identify where the rate basis needs monitoring.

At portfolio level, baseline loss ratio is exactly 60.00% by construction. GLM and credibility both produce a 59.37% observed synthetic loss ratio because credibility preserves the total GLM expected loss. The elevated seller tier remains weak after credibility, with a credibility loss ratio of 81.53%, and should stay on a pricing and underwriting watchlist.

Stress testing

Stress testing evaluates whether the current premiums remain adequate under adverse loss scenarios. The current version does not retrain the model; it applies deterministic frequency and severity multipliers to observed synthetic loss and compares the stressed loss against baseline, GLM, and credibility premium.

Under credibility pricing, a 20% frequency deterioration or a 20% severity deterioration each increases portfolio loss ratio to 71.25%. A combined 20% frequency and 20% severity deterioration increases the loss ratio to 85.50%, which is classified as high pressure. A near_glm seller tier combined stress also creates high pressure because that tier contains most exposures.

Champion-challenger governance

XGBoost is implemented as a challenger model using the same quote-time-known feature philosophy as the GLM. It uses covered_claim_flag for frequency and net loss on covered claims for severity, excludes seller_id as a direct feature, and excludes outcome and synthetic-generation fields to avoid leakage.

The challenger slightly improves test frequency AUC, moving from 0.560 for GLM to 0.561 for XGBoost. That is not enough to change the rate basis. The calibrated XGBoost challenger has weaker test pricing calibration, with a 60.87% test loss ratio versus 60.18% for GLM. In insurance pricing, ranking improvement does not override expected-loss calibration.

Limitations and production requirements

The main limitation is that the claim layer is synthetic. The project demonstrates the actuarial workflow and governance logic, but the numerical rates should not be interpreted as production TikTok Shop insurance rates. Olist is Brazilian e-commerce data, not platform policy, seller, claim, and payment history for the target product.

Production deployment would require real policy, order, return, claim, payment, recovery, expense, seller, and exposure history. It would also need historical training and future validation windows, claim development and IBNR treatment, explicit expense, margin, capital, reinsurance, underwriting, and compliance assumptions. XGBoost should remain in challenger governance until it proves better calibration, not only better ranking.

  1. Replace synthetic claims with real order, return, claim, payment, and recovery records.
  2. Create historical training, validation, monitoring, and out-of-time evaluation windows.
  3. Add expenses, profit margin, risk margin, capital, reinsurance, and compliance constraints.
  4. Define rate-change caps, minimum credibility thresholds, and seller watchlist actions.
  5. Keep GLM + credibility as champion until a challenger wins on calibration and governance.
